Travel to South Africa

The Journey Home

After finishing up the Belgium phase of the project in Hasselt, I began the long journey back to South Africa. My first stop was Eindhoven, where I spent the night catching up with friends we had made earlier in the semester. It was a good way to decompress after the final AI presentation push before starting the long-haul travel. Revisiting Eindhoven felt like coming full circle, as this was where our European journey had begun months earlier.

The next day, I took a train from Eindhoven to Amsterdam. Unfortunately, this part of the trip didn't go as planned; while in Amsterdam, my laptop bag was stolen. It was a massive setback, especially given how much of our project work and personal data is tied to our devices. I had to spend a significant amount of time reporting the theft to the local authorities and trying to sort out the situation before I could head to the airport. The experience was frustrating and stressful, adding an unexpected challenge to what should have been a straightforward travel day.

Despite the stress of the theft, I eventually made it to Schiphol Airport for my flight. I flew from Amsterdam to Doha, where I had a one-day layover. This gave me a bit of time to process the end of the exchange before catching my final flight back to South Africa. The Doha layover provided a moment of quiet reflection amidst the chaos of travel - a chance to think about everything we had accomplished over the past three months across four European countries.

Arriving home after more than three months away was a strange feeling. I was exhausted from the many hours of travel and the loss of my bag, but it was a relief to finally be back. The familiar sights, sounds, and even the smell of South African air were instantly recognizable. There was a sense of completion mixed with the realization that the international portion of our project was over, and now we had the final South African phase to complete.

Returning to South Africa for the final module felt different than arriving in any of the European countries. This wasn't just another destination - it was home, and it was where we would bring together everything we had learned overseas. The contrast between the European winter we had left behind and the South African summer we returned to was striking, mirroring the transition from theoretical development to practical implementation that characterized this final phase of the project.

Weeks in South Africa

My weekly experiences and learnings during the Project Integration & Analysis module

Week 1 in South Africa:

In the first week, the main focus in class was understanding the project and its outputs from different perspectives. We worked specifically on how visualisations can be interpreted by different audiences, such as department officials, scientific or technical audiences, and non-technical audiences like 12-year-old children. This involved looking at how the same data can be communicated in different ways depending on who the information is intended for, and what level of detail and explanation is appropriate.

We discussed what makes a visualisation clear or unclear, including the use of labels, scales, colour, and the amount of information shown at once. These discussions helped us think critically about how our project outputs would eventually be presented and understood by people with very different backgrounds and expectations.

During this week, we also went to Hartbeespoort Dam to deploy the devices that had been built during the Austrian phase of the project. This was the first time the devices were tested in a real outdoor environment. While placing the devices in the water, several technical issues became clear. Powering the devices using laptops or power banks was not practical, as this required the devices to remain close to us. This limited where the devices could be placed and made it clear that an independent power supply would be needed for the devices to float freely on the dam.

We also experienced problems with the GPS sensor. Although the sensor powered on, it was unable to connect to a satellite. This meant that location data could not be collected as intended. Identifying this issue during field testing highlighted the importance of testing all components under real conditions rather than only in controlled environments.

While at the dam, we met our stakeholders in person for the first time. We discussed the current state of the devices and the goals of the project. The stakeholders provided feedback and suggestions for improvement, including the idea of designing a device that could take measurements at multiple depths in the water instead of only just below the surface. This feedback helped clarify future development directions.

The remainder of the week was spent working on the identified technical problems, making adjustments to the devices where possible, and completing Assignment 1, which was based on the work done during this initial phase of the project.

Out of class:

Outside of class, the group had lunch together at Willows on Monday. On Thursday, we drove to Cape Town and spent the weekend there, returning on Monday at the start of Week 2.

In Cape Town, we stayed at Rourke's family farm. On Friday, after the approximately 17-hour drive from Pretoria, the day was mostly spent resting and recovering. We went swimming at Rourke's place and later took a drive into the city centre and the V&A Waterfront.

On Saturday, we did the red bus tour of the city. This allowed us to see several historic parts of Cape Town as well as natural landmarks, including the coastline, beaches, Table Mountain, and Signal Hill. Later that day, we had a sunset picnic at the top of Signal Hill.

On Sunday, we visited additional coastal areas that were not covered during the red bus tour. These included Boulders Beach, where we saw penguins, as well as Misty Cliffs and other nearby locations. On Monday, we drove back to Pretoria.

Week 2 in South Africa:

During the second week, in-class activities focused on analytical methods and reporting frameworks that could be applied to the project. A key topic was the twelve capitals of integrated reporting. We learned what each capital represents and how companies use them to create value. This knowledge was then applied by comparing two companies listed on the Johannesburg Stock Exchange.

The comparison involved looking at how each company performed across the different capitals rather than focusing only on financial results. This helped us understand broader measures of impact and sustainability, and how these factors can influence decision-making in real projects.

We were also introduced to uncertainty analysis and applied these methods to sample datasets. This involved comparing data while taking into account variability and uncertainty, rather than treating values as exact. This process showed how uncertainty can influence interpretations and why it is important to consider when analysing results.

In addition, we created a simple machine learning model as part of the seed count case study. The model was used to generate visualisations that helped show patterns in the data. This activity demonstrated how basic machine learning tools can support analysis and improve how results are presented, even when the models themselves are relatively simple.

Out of class:

Outside of class, we went shopping in Pretoria during the week before driving down to Margate on Thursday.

While in Margate, we spent time at the beaches and went on a rainforest hike. We also visited Oribi Gorge and Lake Eland Nature Reserve. At Lake Eland, we did a game drive, walked across suspension bridges over parts of the gorge, and went on several ziplines, including the fastest zipline in the country, which can reach speeds of up to 160 km/h.

During this trip, we also visited some caves with rock paintings created by the Khoisan people, giving us insight into South Africa's cultural history.

On Sunday, we visited a crocodile park, where we saw crocodiles both in and out of the water, ranging from very young to fully grown. We also saw snakes and were allowed to hold a baby crocodile and a small snake. After this, we went souvenir shopping before starting the roughly nine-hour drive back to Pretoria.

Week 3 in South Africa:

In the third week, the in-class work focused on bringing together everything that had been covered during Weeks 1 and 2. Using the analytical methods, reporting frameworks, and stakeholder considerations, we worked on determining which company would be the best fit for the project.

This process involved comparing the companies based on the criteria developed earlier, including their suitability for the project goals and their potential impact. To support this decision-making process, we worked with a linear programming model, specifically the knapsack model. This model helped structure the decision by balancing constraints and priorities in a systematic way.

After completing the analysis and selecting the most suitable company, we focused on preparing our final presentations. This included organising the results, refining the visualisations and dashboard, and ensuring that the project outcomes were communicated clearly. The final presentations marked the end of the project, which concluded after three months of work across multiple countries.

Out of class:

Outside of class, we visited Pilanesberg Nature Reserve, where we went on a game drive and observed the wildlife.

After the final presentations, we had a farewell braai on Wednesday. This brought not just the South African part of the project to an end but the entire project and gave us a chance to reflect on the experience and the work we had completed as a group.

Intercultural Competencies:

The Netherlands has a culture that is very structured and task oriented. People tend to follow schedules closely, stick to clear roles, and focus on efficiency and precision. Meetings are usually short and to the point, and work is approached with careful planning. Spontaneous ideas and informal social interaction are less common during work time, as the priority is on completing tasks effectively.

In Spain, the culture is more relaxed and social. People take time to connect personally and enjoy conversations before or alongside work. Flexibility is common, and rules are often treated as guidelines rather than strict requirements. This creates a more informal, energetic atmosphere, where creativity and collaboration are encouraged. Decisions are often made through discussion and consensus, rather than following a strict hierarchy or timetable.

South African culture shares similarities with Spain in terms of social connection and flexibility. People value personal interaction, community, and informal communication. Relationships are important in daily life and in teamwork, and there is a balance between following plans and adapting to changing circumstances. Like in Spain, this creates a lively and adaptable environment, where collaboration and problem-solving often involve negotiation and discussion rather than strict rules.

Austria, on the other hand, combines structure and formality with a strong respect for tradition and quality. People value organization, punctuality, and precision, similar to the Netherlands, but with a slightly more formal and hierarchical tone. Work is approached in a systematic and methodical way, and there is a clear separation between professional and personal life. Hierarchies are respected, and decisions typically follow established procedures and expert input rather than spontaneous discussion.

Communication in the Netherlands tends to be direct and straightforward. People value clarity, honesty, and efficiency in how information is shared. In Spain, communication is generally warmer and more expressive. People use conversation to build relationships, and interactions often include humour, gestures, and personal anecdotes. Listening and dialogue are important for reaching agreement. In South Africa, communication is also friendly and relational, often combining directness with warmth and respect. People value mutual understanding and tend to adapt their tone depending on the situation. In Austria, communication is polite, respectful, and often more formal. People choose their words carefully and maintain a courteous tone, especially in professional settings. While honesty is appreciated, it is balanced with diplomacy to avoid conflict or misunderstanding.

Problem-solving and decision-making also differ between these cultures. In the Netherlands, issues are usually addressed quickly and pragmatically, with a focus on practical solutions. In Spain and South Africa, solutions are often negotiated through discussion and compromise, taking into account relationships and group harmony. This allows for flexibility and creativity but can take more time to reach a final decision. Austria leans toward careful analysis and thorough evaluation before acting. Decisions may take longer, as accuracy and long-term stability are prioritized over speed. The Austrian approach emphasizes precision, planning, and maintaining high standards in both process and outcome.

Returning to South Africa after experiencing European cultures gave me new appreciation for the South African approach to collaboration and problem-solving. The balance between structure and flexibility, combined with the emphasis on relationships and community, creates a unique working environment that values both technical excellence and human connection.

Module Overview:

Project Integration & Analysis Process:

This final module focused on integrating and analyzing the complete environmental monitoring system developed across multiple countries. The South African phase represented the culmination of the Global Minor program, bringing together technical implementation, stakeholder engagement, and strategic analysis.

Key Components:

  • Multi-Perspective Analysis: Examining project outputs from viewpoints of technical experts, department officials, and non-technical audiences
  • Field Deployment & Testing: Real-world deployment at Hartbeespoort Dam with identification of practical implementation challenges
  • Integrated Reporting: Application of six capitals framework for comprehensive organizational evaluation
  • Uncertainty Analysis: Consideration of variability and measurement error in data interpretation
  • Decision Modeling: Application of knapsack model for strategic company selection
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Direct interaction with project stakeholders for feedback and alignment

Why

The purpose of this phase was to transition from technical development to practical implementation and strategic decision-making. By testing devices in real conditions, engaging with stakeholders, and applying analytical frameworks, we validated the project's practical feasibility and strategic value. This phase ensured that months of international work culminated in actionable insights and recommendations.

How

We followed an integrated approach:

  1. Field Validation: Deployed IoT devices at Hartbeespoort Dam to identify real-world implementation challenges
  2. Stakeholder Alignment: Engaged with project stakeholders to ensure technical development met practical needs
  3. Analytical Framing: Applied integrated reporting and uncertainty analysis to establish decision criteria
  4. Strategic Decision-Making: Used knapsack model to evaluate and select implementation partners
  5. Multi-Audience Communication: Developed presentations tailored to different stakeholder perspectives

What

By the end of the module, we achieved:

  • Successful field testing of IoT devices with identification of key implementation challenges
  • Direct stakeholder feedback incorporated into project recommendations
  • Comprehensive analytical framework for evaluating project options
  • Strategic company selection based on multiple criteria beyond financial considerations
  • Clear communication strategy for diverse stakeholder audiences
  • Completion of a three-month international project spanning four European countries and South Africa

This module represented my first experience with end-to-end project integration from technical development through to strategic implementation. I learned how technical excellence must be complemented by stakeholder alignment, practical feasibility, and strategic analysis to create meaningful impact. The experience of completing this international program has fundamentally expanded my understanding of global collaboration, cross-cultural project management, and sustainable technology implementation.
